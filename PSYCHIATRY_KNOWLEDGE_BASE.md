## 1. Foundations of Modern Psychiatry

### 1.1 Brief History

```
Timeline of Psychiatry
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Ancient Era (BCE-500 CE)
  ├── Hippocrates: "brain is seat of mental illness" (~400 BCE)
  ├── Galen: temperament theory (sanguine, choleric, melancholic, phlegmatic)
  └── Trepanation practiced across civilizations

Middle Ages (500-1400)
  ├── Demonological model dominates
  ├── Islamic Golden Age: first psychiatric hospitals (Bimaristan, Baghdad ~705 CE)
  └── Avicenna's Canon of Medicine: melancholia as brain disorder

Asylum Era (1400-1800)
  ├── Bethlem Royal Hospital ("Bedlam") est. 1247, notorious by 1600s
  ├── Moral Treatment movement: Philippe Pinel (1793) unchains patients
  └── Dorothea Dix (1840s): reform of US asylums

Early Modern (1800-1950)
  ├── Emil Kraepelin (1856-1926): classification (dementia praecox vs manic depression)
  ├── Sigmund Freud (1856-1939): psychoanalysis, unconscious mind
  ├── Eugen Bleuler (1911): coins "schizophrenia"
  ├── Hans Berger (1929): first human EEG
  ├── Insulin coma therapy (1927), lobotomy (1935), ECT (1938)
  └── DSM-I published (1952): 106 disorders

Biological Revolution (1950-2000)
  ├── Chlorpromazine (1952): first antipsychotic
  ├── Imipramine (1957): first tricyclic antidepressant
  ├── Lithium (1949/FDA 1970): mood stabilization
  ├── Benzodiazepines (1960s): chlordiazepoxide
  ├── Fluoxetine / Prozac (1987): SSRI revolution
  ├── DSM-III (1980): atheoretical, criterion-based diagnosis
  ├── Neuroimaging: CT (1970s), MRI (1980s), fMRI (1990s), PET (1970s)
  └── Human Genome Project launched (1990)

Precision Psychiatry Era (2000-present)
  ├── GWAS era: polygenicity of mental illness revealed
  ├── RDoC framework (NIMH, 2010)
  ├── Ketamine/esketamine for TRD (FDA 2019)
  ├── Psychedelic renaissance: psilocybin, MDMA research
  ├── Digital psychiatry: apps, wearables, telepsychiatry
  ├── DSM-5 (2013) → DSM-5-TR (2022)
  └── Computational psychiatry and AI-assisted diagnosis
```

### 1.5 Research Domain Criteria (RDoC) — NIMH Framework

RDoC was launched in 2010 by NIMH director Thomas Insel to move beyond categorical diagnosis toward dimensional, biologically-grounded constructs.

```
RDoC Matrix
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

DOMAINS (rows):                    UNITS OF ANALYSIS (columns):
                                   Genes → Molecules → Cells →
                                   Circuits → Physiology →
                                   Behavior → Self-Report →
                                   Paradigms

1. Negative Valence Systems
   ├── Acute threat (fear)
   ├── Potential threat (anxiety)
   ├── Sustained threat
   ├── Loss
   └── Frustrative nonreward

2. Positive Valence Systems
   ├── Reward responsiveness
   ├── Reward learning
   ├── Reward valuation
   └── Effort valuation/willingness to work

3. Cognitive Systems
   ├── Attention
   ├── Perception
   ├── Declarative memory
   ├── Language
   ├── Cognitive control
   └── Working memory

4. Social Processes
   ├── Affiliation and attachment
   ├── Social communication
   ├── Perception/understanding of self
   └── Perception/understanding of others

5. Arousal and Regulatory Systems
   ├── Arousal
   ├── Circadian rhythms
   └── Sleep-wakefulness

6. Sensorimotor Systems
   ├── Motor actions
   ├── Agency and ownership
   ├── Habit
   ├── Innate motor patterns
   └── Sensorimotor dynamics
```

### 1.6 Dimensional vs Categorical Approaches

| Aspect | Categorical (DSM/ICD) | Dimensional (RDoC/HiTOP) |
|--------|----------------------|---------------------------|
| Diagnosis | Present or absent | Severity on a continuum |
| Comorbidity | Common (artifact of boundaries) | Expected (shared dimensions) |
| Treatment matching | Disorder → protocol | Symptom dimension → targeted intervention |
| Research utility | Clinical trials, epidemiology | Mechanistic studies, biomarker discovery |
| Clinical utility | Insurance billing, communication | Personalized treatment planning |
| Limitation | Heterogeneity within categories | Not yet validated for clinical use |

**HiTOP (Hierarchical Taxonomy of Psychopathology)** — empirically derived dimensional model:
```
Superspectra:  General psychopathology ("p factor")
                        │
Spectra:     ┌──────────┼──────────┬──────────┬──────────┐
             │          │          │          │          │
          Internalizing Thought  Externalizing Detachment Somatoform
             │        Disorder      │
          ┌──┴──┐       │       ┌──┴──┐
        Distress Fear  Psychosis Antisocial Substance
```

## 2. Neuroanatomy of Psychiatric Illness

### 2.1 Key Brain Regions

| Region | Location | Function | Psychiatric Relevance |
|--------|----------|----------|----------------------|
| **Prefrontal Cortex (PFC)** | Anterior frontal lobe | Executive function, decision-making, working memory, impulse control | Hypofunction in ADHD, depression, schizophrenia; damage → disinhibition |
| **Dorsolateral PFC (dlPFC)** | Lateral surface of PFC | Working memory, cognitive flexibility, planning | Target for TMS in depression; hypoactive in MDD |
| **Ventromedial PFC (vmPFC)** | Medial inferior PFC | Emotion regulation, reward valuation, fear extinction | Lesions → impaired decision-making; hypoactive in PTSD |
| **Orbitofrontal Cortex (OFC)** | Above orbits | Reward processing, behavioral inhibition | OCD (hyperactive), antisocial PD (hypoactive) |
| **Anterior Cingulate Cortex (ACC)** | Medial frontal, above corpus callosum | Error detection, conflict monitoring, emotional pain | Subgenual ACC (area 25) overactive in depression; DBS target |
| **Amygdala** | Medial temporal lobe | Fear processing, emotional memory, threat detection | Hyperactive in anxiety, PTSD; hypoactive in psychopathy |
| **Hippocampus** | Medial temporal lobe | Declarative memory, spatial navigation, contextual fear | Volume loss in PTSD, depression, schizophrenia; stress-sensitive |
| **Insula** | Deep to lateral sulcus | Interoception, disgust, empathy, pain perception | Anxiety (hyperactive); addiction (craving); eating disorders |
| **Basal Ganglia** | Subcortical nuclei | Motor control, habit formation, reward learning | OCD (caudate), ADHD (striatum), Tourette's, schizophrenia |
| **Nucleus Accumbens (NAcc)** | Ventral striatum | Reward, pleasure, motivation | Addiction (dopamine surge), anhedonia in depression (hypoactive) |
| **Thalamus** | Diencephalon, central relay | Sensory relay, consciousness, arousal | Schizophrenia (filtering deficits); sleep disorders |
| **Hypothalamus** | Below thalamus | HPA axis, autonomic function, circadian rhythms, appetite | Stress response, eating disorders, sleep disorders |
| **Cerebellum** | Posterior fossa | Motor coordination, cognitive timing, emotion modulation | Emerging role in schizophrenia, autism, ADHD |
| **Brainstem (LC, VTA, raphe)** | Midbrain/pons/medulla | Monoamine production, arousal, autonomic regulation | Source of DA (VTA), NE (LC), 5-HT (raphe nuclei) |

### 2.3 Key Neural Circuits

| Circuit | Core Regions | Normal Function | Dysfunction → |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Fear Circuit** | Amygdala → hypothalamus → PAG → brainstem | Threat detection, fight-or-flight | Anxiety, PTSD, phobias |
| **Reward Circuit** | VTA → NAcc → mPFC | Motivation, pleasure, reinforcement learning | Addiction, anhedonia, gambling disorder |
| **Default Mode Network (DMN)** | mPFC, PCC, angular gyrus, hippocampus | Self-referential thought, rumination, mind-wandering | Overactive in depression (rumination); disrupted in schizophrenia |
| **Salience Network (SN)** | Anterior insula, dACC | Detecting relevant stimuli, switching between DMN and CEN | Anxiety (hyperactive), schizophrenia (misattribution of salience) |
| **Central Executive Network (CEN)** | dlPFC, posterior parietal cortex | Working memory, attention, problem-solving | Hypoactive in ADHD, depression, schizophrenia |
| **CSTC Loop** | Cortex → striatum → globus pallidus → thalamus → cortex | Habit formation, motor planning, cognitive filtering | OCD (overactive direct pathway), Tourette's, ADHD |
| **Emotion Regulation** | vlPFC/dlPFC → amygdala (top-down inhibition) | Cognitive reappraisal, affect regulation | BPD (weak top-down), PTSD, MDD |
| **Social Brain** | TPJ, STS, mPFC, fusiform face area | Theory of mind, face processing, empathy | Autism (TPJ/STS), psychopathy (amygdala/vmPFC) |

### 2.4 Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB) and Drug Delivery

```
Blood  ──►  Endothelial cells (tight junctions)  ──►  Brain parenchyma
             │
             ├── Lipophilic drugs cross easily (benzodiazepines, antipsychotics)
             ├── Hydrophilic drugs need transporters (L-DOPA via LAT1)
             ├── P-glycoprotein efflux pump removes many drugs
             ├── Inflammation can transiently open BBB
             └── Circumventricular organs lack BBB (area postrema → nausea)

Clinical implications:
  - Most psychotropics must be lipophilic to cross BBB
  - Lithium (small ion) crosses via sodium channels
  - Large molecules (antibodies) generally excluded → limits biologics
  - BBB breakdown in neurodegeneration may accelerate disease
```

| Modality | Measures | Spatial Res | Temporal Res | Psychiatric Use |
|----------|----------|-------------|--------------|-----------------|
| **Structural MRI** | Brain anatomy, volume | ~1 mm | N/A (static) | Hippocampal atrophy (depression, PTSD), ventricular enlargement (schizophrenia) |
| **fMRI (BOLD)** | Blood oxygenation (proxy for neural activity) | 2-3 mm | 1-2 sec | Task activation, resting-state connectivity, DMN studies |
| **DTI/DWI** | White matter tract integrity | 2 mm | N/A | Disconnection in schizophrenia, TBI, corpus callosum abnormalities |

## 3. Neurotransmitter Systems

### 3.1 Monoamine Systems

#### Serotonin (5-Hydroxytryptamine, 5-HT)

```
Synthesis Pathway:
  Tryptophan (essential amino acid, from diet)
      │
      ▼  [Tryptophan hydroxylase (TPH1/TPH2)] ← rate-limiting step
  5-Hydroxytryptophan (5-HTP)
      │
      ▼  [Aromatic amino acid decarboxylase (AADC)]
  SEROTONIN (5-HT)
      │
      ▼  [Monoamine oxidase A (MAO-A)]
  5-Hydroxyindoleacetic acid (5-HIAA) → excreted

Source: Dorsal & median raphe nuclei (brainstem)
Projections: → cortex, limbic system, hypothalamus, spinal cord
~95% of body's serotonin is in the GUT (enterochromaffin cells)
```

| Receptor | Location | Function | Drug Target |
|----------|----------|----------|-------------|
| **5-HT1A** | Raphe (autoreceptor), hippocampus, cortex | Anxiolysis, mood; presynaptic = inhibits 5-HT release | Buspirone (partial agonist), vilazodone; SSRI desensitization |
| **5-HT1B/1D** | Cranial blood vessels, basal ganglia | Vasoconstriction, autoreceptor | Triptans (migraine) |
| **5-HT2A** | Cortex (layer V pyramidal), platelets | Hallucinations, platelet aggregation, sleep | Atypical antipsychotics (antagonist); psilocybin (agonist) |
| **5-HT2C** | Choroid plexus, hypothalamus | Appetite, mood, anxiety | Lorcaserin (weight loss, withdrawn); antipsychotic weight gain (blockade) |
| **5-HT3** | Area postrema, vagus nerve, GI tract | Nausea/vomiting | Ondansetron (antiemetic) |
| **5-HT4** | GI tract, hippocampus | GI motility, cognition | Prucalopride (constipation) |
| **5-HT6** | Cortex, hippocampus, striatum | Cognition, memory | Investigational procognitive agents |
| **5-HT7** | Hypothalamus, thalamus, hippocampus | Circadian rhythm, mood, cognition | Lurasidone (high affinity antagonist) |

#### Dopamine (DA)

```
Synthesis Pathway:
  Tyrosine (amino acid, from diet or phenylalanine)
      │
      ▼  [Tyrosine hydroxylase (TH)] ← rate-limiting step
  L-DOPA (L-3,4-dihydroxyphenylalanine)
      │
      ▼  [AADC / DOPA decarboxylase]
  DOPAMINE (DA)
      │
      ▼  [Dopamine β-hydroxylase (DBH)] ← only in NE neurons
  NOREPINEPHRINE (NE)
      │
      ▼  [Phenylethanolamine N-methyltransferase (PNMT)] ← only in adrenal
  EPINEPHRINE

Degradation: MAO-B and COMT → HVA (homovanillic acid)
```

**Four Major Dopamine Pathways:**

```
1. MESOLIMBIC:     VTA ──────► Nucleus Accumbens / Limbic
                   │           Reward, motivation, pleasure
                   │           OVERACTIVE → positive symptoms of psychosis
                   │
2. MESOCORTICAL:   VTA ──────► Prefrontal Cortex
                   │           Working memory, cognition, motivation
                   │           UNDERACTIVE → negative/cognitive symptoms
                   │
3. NIGROSTRIATAL:  Substantia ► Dorsal Striatum (caudate/putamen)
                   Nigra        Motor control, habit formation
                   │            DEGENERATION → Parkinson's disease
                   │            BLOCKADE → EPS (antipsychotics)
                   │
4. TUBEROINFUNDIBULAR: Hypothalamus ► Pituitary
                       │              Inhibits prolactin release
                       │              BLOCKADE → hyperprolactinemia
                       │              (galactorrhea, amenorrhea)
```

| Receptor | Coupling | Location | Function | Drugs |
|----------|----------|----------|----------|-------|
| **D1** | Gs (excitatory) | Cortex, striatum | Cognition, motor | D1 agonists (investigational procognitive) |
| **D2** | Gi (inhibitory) | Striatum, limbic, pituitary | Motor, reward, prolactin | ALL antipsychotics block D2; agonists = Parkinson's Rx |
| **D3** | Gi | NAcc, VTA | Reward, mood | Cariprazine (D3-preferring partial agonist) |
| **D4** | Gi | Cortex, amygdala | Attention, cognition | Clozapine (high D4 affinity) |
| **D5** | Gs | Hippocampus, hypothalamus | Cognition | Limited pharmacological targeting |

#### Norepinephrine (NE)

```
Source: Locus coeruleus (LC, brainstem) — only ~50,000 neurons per side
Projections: → entire cortex, cerebellum, hippocampus, amygdala, spinal cord

Functions:
  ├── Arousal and wakefulness
  ├── Attention and focus
  ├── Fight-or-flight (sympathetic activation)
  ├── Mood regulation
  └── Pain modulation (descending pathways)

Receptors:
  α1: postsynaptic → arousal, vasoconstriction (prazosin blocks → PTSD nightmares)
  α2: presynaptic autoreceptor → inhibits NE release (clonidine, guanfacine → ADHD)
  β1: heart (↑rate, ↑contractility) → propranolol blocks (performance anxiety)
  β2: bronchi, vasculature → bronchodilation

Dysregulation:
  ↑ NE → anxiety, panic, PTSD hyperarousal, mania
  ↓ NE → depression (fatigue, poor concentration), ADHD
```

### 3.2 Amino Acid Neurotransmitters

#### GABA (Gamma-Aminobutyric Acid) — Primary Inhibitory

```
Glutamate ──[GAD (glutamic acid decarboxylase)]──► GABA

GABA-A receptor: ligand-gated Cl⁻ channel (fast inhibition)
  ├── Benzodiazepine binding site → ↑ frequency of Cl⁻ channel opening
  ├── Barbiturate binding site → ↑ duration of Cl⁻ channel opening
  ├── Alcohol binding site → potentiates GABA-A
  ├── Neurosteroid site → allopregnanolone (brexanolone for PPD)
  └── Subunit composition determines pharmacology (α1=sedation, α2/3=anxiolysis)

GABA-B receptor: G-protein coupled (slow inhibition)
  └── Baclofen (agonist) — spasticity, investigational for addiction

Psychiatric relevance:
  ↓ GABA → anxiety, seizures, insomnia
  GABAergic drugs → benzodiazepines, Z-drugs, barbiturates, gabapentin, pregabalin
  MRS studies: ↓ GABA in depression, anxiety, PTSD
```

#### Glutamate — Primary Excitatory

```
Glutamate is the most abundant neurotransmitter in the brain (~60% of synapses)

Key Receptors:
  IONOTROPIC (fast):
    ├── NMDA (N-methyl-D-aspartate)
    │     ├── Requires glycine co-agonist + depolarization (Mg²⁺ plug)
    │     ├── Ca²⁺ influx → synaptic plasticity (LTP)
    │     ├── Ketamine = NMDA antagonist → rapid antidepressant
    │     ├── PCP, MK-801 = NMDA antagonists → psychosis model
    │     └── Memantine = weak antagonist (Alzheimer's)
    │
    ├── AMPA (α-amino-3-hydroxy-5-methyl-4-isoxazolepropionic acid)
    │     └── Fast excitation; AMPAkines → investigational cognitive enhancers
    │
    └── Kainate
          └── Modulation of GABA release; epilepsy models

  METABOTROPIC (slow, G-protein coupled):
    ├── Group I (mGluR1, 5): postsynaptic, excitatory
    ├── Group II (mGluR2, 3): presynaptic, inhibitory (autoreceptors)
    │     └── mGluR2/3 agonists → investigational for schizophrenia, anxiety
    └── Group III (mGluR4, 6, 7, 8): presynaptic, inhibitory

Glutamate hypothesis of schizophrenia:
  NMDA receptor hypofunction → disinhibition of glutamate release
  → downstream dopamine dysregulation
  PCP/ketamine in healthy subjects mimics positive AND negative symptoms
```

### 3.3 Acetylcholine (ACh)

```
Synthesis: Choline + Acetyl-CoA ──[ChAT]──► Acetylcholine
Degradation: AChE (acetylcholinesterase) → choline + acetate

Cholinergic nuclei:
  ├── Nucleus basalis of Meynert → cortex (degenerated in Alzheimer's)
  ├── Medial septum → hippocampus (memory)
  ├── Pedunculopontine/LDT → thalamus (arousal, REM sleep)
  └── Striatal interneurons → motor control

Receptors:
  Nicotinic (nAChR): ligand-gated ion channels
    ├── α4β2: most common brain subtype → nicotine addiction target
    ├── α7: hippocampus → cognition, sensory gating (deficient in schizophrenia)
    └── Varenicline: α4β2 partial agonist (smoking cessation)

  Muscarinic (mAChR): G-protein coupled
    ├── M1: cortex, hippocampus → cognition (xanomeline, KarXT for schizophrenia)
    ├── M2: autoreceptor → inhibits ACh release
    ├── M3: GI, bladder, salivary → anticholinergic side effects
    └── Anticholinergic burden → cognitive impairment, delirium in elderly
```

### 3.4 Endocannabinoid System

```
Endocannabinoids: anandamide (AEA), 2-arachidonoylglycerol (2-AG)
  ├── Synthesized ON-DEMAND (not stored in vesicles)
  ├── Act as RETROGRADE messengers (postsynaptic → presynaptic)
  └── Degraded by FAAH (for AEA) and MAGL (for 2-AG)

Receptors:
  CB1: brain (cortex, hippocampus, basal ganglia, cerebellum)
    └── Inhibits neurotransmitter release (both GABA and glutamate)
  CB2: immune cells, microglia → neuroinflammation modulation

Psychiatric relevance:
  ├── Cannabis: THC = CB1 partial agonist → euphoria, anxiety, psychosis risk
  ├── CBD: negative allosteric modulator of CB1, 5-HT1A agonist → anxiolytic
  ├── Anandamide ↓ in PTSD, ↑ in schizophrenia (cerebrospinal fluid)
  └── FAAH inhibitors: investigational anxiolytics
```

### 3.5 Neuropeptides

### 3.7 Neuroplasticity and BDNF

```
BDNF (Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor)
  ├── Most abundant neurotrophin in the brain
  ├── Produced in hippocampus, cortex, basal forebrain
  ├── Binds TrkB receptor → promotes:
  │     ├── Neuronal survival
  │     ├── Synaptic plasticity (LTP)

  │     ├── Neurogenesis (hippocampal dentate gyrus)
  │     └── Synaptogenesis
  │

  │     ├── Val/Val: normal BDNF secretion
  │     ├── Val/Met or Met/Met: ↓ activity-dependent BDNF secretion

        Antidepressants → ↑ BDNF → ↑ neurogenesis → recovery (takes weeks)
```

### 4.4 Polygenic Risk Scores (PRS)

```
PRS Construction:
  1. Run GWAS in discovery sample (N = 100,000+)
  2. Identify all SNPs with p < threshold (e.g., p < 0.05, or genome-wide)
  3. Weight each SNP by its effect size (log OR or beta)
  4. Sum weighted allele counts in target individual
  5. PRS = Σ (βᵢ × alleleᵢ)

Current Predictive Power (AUC / variance explained):
  ├── Schizophrenia PRS: explains ~7-8% of variance (AUC ~0.70)
  ├── Bipolar PRS: ~4-5% variance
  ├── Depression PRS: ~2-3% variance
  ├── ADHD PRS: ~5% variance
  └── For comparison: 80% heritability means theoretical max ~80%

Clinical utility (as of 2024):
  ├── NOT diagnostic (too low predictive power for individual-level)
  ├── Population-level risk stratification (research)
  ├── Prediction of treatment response (emerging)
  ├── Cross-disorder prediction (MDD PRS predicts anxiety too)
  └── Combined with clinical data → better prediction than either alone
```

## 5. Depressive Disorders

### 5.1 Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)

**Epidemiology:**
- Lifetime prevalence: ~17-20% (one of the most common psychiatric disorders)
- Female:Male ratio: ~2:1
- Mean age of onset: ~25-30 years (bimodal: late teens and late 40s-50s)
- Leading cause of disability worldwide (WHO, 2023)
- Recurrence rate: 50% after first episode, 70% after second, 90% after third

**DSM-5-TR Diagnostic Criteria:**

At least **5 of 9 symptoms** present during the same 2-week period, representing a change from previous functioning. At least one must be (1) or (2):

```
Mnemonic: SIG E CAPS (prescribe energy capsules)

1. Sleep disturbance (insomnia or hypersomnia, nearly every day)
2. Interest loss / anhedonia (markedly diminished interest or pleasure) *
3. Guilt (excessive or inappropriate guilt, feelings of worthlessness)
4. Energy loss (fatigue, nearly every day)
5. Concentration difficulty (diminished ability to think, indecisiveness)
6. Appetite change (decrease or increase; weight change >5% in a month)
7. Psychomotor agitation or retardation (observable by others)
8. Suicidal ideation (recurrent thoughts of death, SI, plan, or attempt)
9. Depressed mood (subjective or observed) *

* At least one of these required
```

**Exclusions:** Not attributable to substance/medical condition; no history of manic/hypomanic episode (→ bipolar); not better explained by schizoaffective/schizophrenia spectrum.

**Severity Specifiers:**
| Severity | Symptoms | Functional Impairment |
|----------|----------|----------------------|
| Mild | 5 symptoms, minor impairment | Can still work/socialize with effort |
| Moderate | 6-7 symptoms | Significant difficulty functioning |
| Severe | 8-9 symptoms, marked impairment | Unable to function in major roles |

### 5.2 MDD Subtypes

| Subtype | Key Features | Treatment Implications |
|---------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Melancholic** | Anhedonia (pervasive), worse in morning, early morning awakening, psychomotor change, anorexia, excessive guilt | Better response to TCAs, ECT; biological correlates strong |
| **Atypical** | Mood reactivity, hypersomnia, leaden paralysis, hyperphagia/weight gain, interpersonal rejection sensitivity | MAOIs historically effective; SSRIs first-line |
| **Psychotic** | Mood-congruent delusions (guilt, worthlessness, nihilism, somatic) or hallucinations | Requires antidepressant + antipsychotic; ECT highly effective |
| **Seasonal (SAD)** | Onset fall/winter, remission spring/summer; hypersomnia, carb craving, weight gain | Light therapy (10,000 lux, 30 min morning); SSRIs; bupropion XL (prevention) |
| **Peripartum** | Onset during pregnancy or within 4 weeks postpartum | SSRIs (sertraline safest in breastfeeding); brexanolone IV for severe; screen with EPDS |
| **Persistent (Dysthymia)** | Depressed mood more days than not for ≥2 years; ≥2 additional symptoms | Chronic → combine medication + psychotherapy; may require longer treatment |
| **With anxious distress** | Tense, restless, difficulty concentrating due to worry, fear of losing control | May predict slower treatment response; consider SSRI/SNRI + buspirone |
| **With mixed features** | Meets MDD criteria + ≥3 manic symptoms simultaneously | Avoid antidepressant monotherapy → risk of switching; mood stabilizer |

### 5.3 Neurobiology of Depression

```
EVOLVING MODELS OF DEPRESSION
══════════════════════════════

1. MONOAMINE HYPOTHESIS (1960s-1990s)
   ├── Depression = ↓ serotonin, norepinephrine, and/or dopamine
   ├── Evidence: reserpine (depletes monoamines) → depression
   ├── All first-gen antidepressants increase monoamine availability
   ├── LIMITATION: monoamine increase is immediate, but clinical
   │   response takes 2-4 weeks → something else must be happening
   └── Status: necessary but insufficient explanation

2. NEUROPLASTICITY / NEUROTROPHIC HYPOTHESIS (2000s)
   ├── Depression = ↓ BDNF → ↓ hippocampal neurogenesis
   ├── Stress → ↑ cortisol → hippocampal atrophy (MRI confirmed)
   ├── Antidepressants → ↑ BDNF → restore plasticity (takes weeks)
   ├── Ketamine → rapid synaptogenesis via mTOR pathway
   └── Status: strong evidence, explains therapeutic delay

3. NEUROINFLAMMATION HYPOTHESIS (2010s)
   ├── Subgroup of MDD patients (~30%) have ↑ CRP, IL-6, TNF-α
   ├── Sickness behavior mimics depression (fatigue, anhedonia, social withdrawal)
   ├── Interferon-alpha therapy → depression in 30-50% of patients
   ├── Anti-inflammatory agents (celecoxib, minocycline) → adjunctive benefit
   └── Status: relevant for inflammatory subtype; precision medicine target

4. GLUTAMATE / SYNAPTIC HYPOTHESIS (2010s-present)
   ├── Ketamine (NMDA antagonist) → rapid antidepressant in hours
   ├── Mechanism: blocks NMDA on GABAergic interneurons → disinhibits
   │   glutamate → ↑ AMPA activation → BDNF release → synaptogenesis
   ├── Prefrontal synaptic deficits reversed rapidly
   └── Status: paradigm shift; led to FDA approval of esketamine (2019)

5. NETWORK / CIRCUIT HYPOTHESIS (current)
   ├── Depression = dysconnectivity between brain networks
   ├── DMN hyperconnectivity → rumination
   ├── CEN hypoconnectivity → cognitive deficits
   ├── Salience network imbalance → misweighting of internal vs external stimuli
   ├── Subgenual ACC (area 25) hyperactivity → sadness, DBS target
   └── Status: most integrative model; supports personalized neuromodulation
```

| **Sertraline** | SSRI | 5-HT reuptake + mild DA reuptake | 50→50-200 mg | 26 h | GI (diarrhea), sexual dysfunction | Best evidence in cardiac patients (SADHART); safest in breastfeeding |
| **Escitalopram** | SSRI | Most selective 5-HT reuptake | 10→10-20 mg | 27-33 h | Sexual dysfunction, QTc (>20 mg) | S-enantiomer of citalopram; may have fastest onset |
| **Paroxetine** | SSRI | 5-HT reuptake + mild NE + anticholinergic | 20→20-50 mg | 21 h | Weight gain, sedation, sexual dysfunction, discontinuation syndrome | Worst for teratogenicity (Pregnancy Cat D); hardest SSRI to stop |
| **Venlafaxine** | SNRI | 5-HT (low dose) + NE (>150 mg) | 75→75-375 mg | 5 h (ODV: 11 h) | Hypertension (dose-dependent), nausea, discontinuation | May be more effective at higher doses; monitor BP |
| **Duloxetine** | SNRI | 5-HT + NE reuptake | 30→60-120 mg | 12 h | Nausea, dry mouth, constipation, hepatotoxicity risk | Also for neuropathic pain, fibromyalgia, GAD |
| **Bupropion** | NDRI | NE + DA reuptake | 150→300-450 mg | 21 h | Insomnia, agitation, seizure risk (>450 mg) | NO sexual dysfunction; NO weight gain; smoking cessation; avoid in eating disorders/seizures |
| **Mirtazapine** | NaSSA | α2 antagonist + 5-HT2A/2C/3 blockade + H1 | 15→15-45 mg | 20-40 h | Weight gain, sedation (paradoxically less at higher doses), ↑ appetite | Good for insomnia, poor appetite; less sexual dysfunction |
| **Esketamine** | NMDA antagonist | NMDA blockade → glutamate surge → BDNF | 56-84 mg intranasal | 7-12 h | Dissociation, sedation, ↑ BP, nausea | Spravato REMS: administered in clinic, 2h monitoring; for TRD or MDD with suicidal ideation |
| **Brexanolone** | Neurosteroid | GABA-A positive allosteric modulator | 60h IV infusion | 9 h | Sedation, loss of consciousness | Zulresso; for postpartum depression; inpatient 60h infusion |

### 5.6 Treatment Algorithm (Stepped Care)

```
STEP 1: Mild depression
  ├── Active monitoring / watchful waiting
  ├── Exercise prescription (150 min/week moderate)
  ├── Sleep hygiene, behavioral activation
  └── Guided self-help (CBT-based)

STEP 2: Moderate depression (first-line)
  ├── SSRI (escitalopram or sertraline = best evidence)
  ├── OR CBT / behavioral activation (6-20 sessions)
  ├── OR combination SSRI + CBT (superior to either alone)
  └── Allow 4-6 weeks for adequate trial; optimize dose

STEP 3: No response after adequate trial
  ├── Switch within class (SSRI → different SSRI)
  ├── Switch across class (SSRI → SNRI or bupropion)
  ├── Augment: add lithium (300-600 mg), aripiprazole (2-5 mg), or buspirone
  ├── Combine: SSRI + bupropion or SSRI + mirtazapine ("California rocket fuel")
  └── Reassess diagnosis (bipolar? substance use? medical? personality?)

STEP 4: Treatment-Resistant Depression (TRD) — failed ≥2 adequate trials
  ├── Esketamine (Spravato) nasal spray
  ├── TMS (transcranial magnetic stimulation) — FDA-cleared for TRD
  ├── ECT (electroconvulsive therapy) — most effective antidepressant (~60-80%)
  ├── MAOIs (phenelzine, tranylcypromine) — dietary restrictions
  └── Combination strategies

STEP 5: Severe / refractory
  ├── ECT maintenance
  ├── VNS (vagus nerve stimulation) — long-term implant
  ├── DBS (investigational — targeting area 25)
  └── Psilocybin-assisted therapy (investigational)

MAINTENANCE:
  ├── First episode: continue antidepressant 6-9 months after remission
  ├── Second episode: 1-2 years
  ├── Third+ episode or severe: indefinite (lifelong)
  └── Taper slowly (over weeks-months) to avoid discontinuation syndrome
```

### 5.7 Other Depressive Disorders

**Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia):**
- Depressed mood more days than not for ≥2 years (≥1 year in children/adolescents)
- ≥2 of: appetite change, sleep change, low energy, low self-esteem, poor concentration, hopelessness
- Never symptom-free for >2 months during the 2-year period
- Can co-occur with MDD episodes ("double depression")

**Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder (PMDD):**
- ≥5 symptoms in the luteal phase, remitting after menses
- Must include ≥1: marked affective lability, irritability/anger, depressed mood, anxiety/tension
- Affects ~3-8% of menstruating women
- Treatment: SSRIs (continuous or luteal-phase dosing), combined oral contraceptives

**Disruptive Mood Dysregulation Disorder (DMDD):**
- Ages 6-18; chronic severe irritability + temper outbursts ≥3x/week
- Created to reduce overdiagnosis of pediatric bipolar disorder

## 6. Anxiety Disorders

### 6.1 Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

**Epidemiology:** Lifetime prevalence ~6%; F:M = 2:1; mean onset ~30 years; highly comorbid with MDD (62%)

**DSM-5-TR Criteria:**
- Excessive anxiety and worry about multiple domains, more days than not, for ≥6 months
- Difficulty controlling worry
- ≥3 of 6 associated symptoms (1 in children):

```
1. Restlessness or feeling keyed up/on edge
2. Easy fatigability
3. Difficulty concentrating / mind going blank
4. Irritability
5. Muscle tension
6. Sleep disturbance
```

**Neurobiology:**
- Amygdala hyperactivation to uncertain/ambiguous threat
- Prefrontal cortex hypofunction (impaired top-down regulation)
- BNST (bed nucleus of stria terminalis): sustained anxiety vs amygdala = acute fear
- GABAergic deficit in amygdala and cortex
- Intolerance of uncertainty (cognitive model — Dugas)

**Treatment:**
| Modality | Options | Evidence Level |
|----------|---------|---------------|
| First-line medication | SSRIs (escitalopram, sertraline, paroxetine), SNRIs (venlafaxine, duloxetine) | A |
| Second-line medication | Buspirone (5-HT1A partial agonist), pregabalin (Europe), hydroxyzine | A/B |
| Psychotherapy | CBT (worry exposure, cognitive restructuring, relaxation training) | A |
| Adjunctive | Benzodiazepines (short-term only; risk of dependence) | B (limited duration) |

### 6.2 Panic Disorder

**Epidemiology:** Lifetime prevalence ~3-5%; F:M = 2:1; onset typically late teens to mid-30s

**Panic Attack Criteria (≥4 of 13 symptoms, peaking within minutes):**

```
PHYSICAL:                          COGNITIVE:
1.  Palpitations / ↑ HR            10. Derealization / depersonalization
2.  Sweating                        11. Fear of losing control / "going crazy"
3.  Trembling / shaking             12. Fear of dying
4.  Shortness of breath             13. Paresthesias (numbness/tingling)
5.  Chest pain / discomfort
6.  Nausea / abdominal distress
7.  Dizziness / lightheadedness
8.  Chills or hot flushes
9.  Choking sensation

Panic DISORDER requires recurrent UNEXPECTED attacks
+ ≥1 month of concern about additional attacks OR maladaptive behavioral change
```

**Fear Network Model (Gorman et al.):**
```
Brainstem (locus coeruleus, PAG)     ──► Physical symptoms
     │                                    (↑HR, ↑breathing, sweating)
Central nucleus of amygdala          ──► Fear / dread
     │    ◄── Misinterpretation of bodily sensations
Hypothalamus                         ──► HPA activation (cortisol)
Prefrontal cortex (normally inhibits) ──► WEAK in panic disorder
```

**Treatment:** SSRI/SNRI + CBT with interoceptive exposure (deliberately inducing symptoms to extinguish fear)

|----------|-----------|-------|-----|---------------------|-------------|

## 7. Trauma and Stressor-Related Disorders

### 7.1 Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

**Epidemiology:**
- ~60-70% of people experience a traumatic event; ~6-8% develop PTSD (lifetime)
- Women: ~10-12% lifetime; Men: ~5-6% (despite more trauma exposure in men)
- Highest rates: rape (49% PTSD), combat veterans (10-30%), mass shooting survivors

**DSM-5-TR Criteria:**

```
CRITERION A: Exposure to actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence
  ├── Direct experience
  ├── Witnessing in person
  ├── Learning about event happening to close family member/friend
  └── Repeated exposure to aversive details (e.g., first responders; NOT via media)

CRITERION B: Intrusion symptoms (≥1 of 5)
  ├── Intrusive distressing memories
  ├── Distressing dreams
  ├── Dissociative reactions (flashbacks)
  ├── Intense psychological distress at reminders
  └── Marked physiological reactions to reminders

CRITERION C: Avoidance (≥1 of 2)
  ├── Avoidance of distressing memories, thoughts, feelings
  └── Avoidance of external reminders (people, places, situations)

CRITERION D: Negative cognition and mood (≥2 of 7)
  ├── Inability to remember key aspect of trauma (dissociative amnesia)
  ├── Persistent negative beliefs about self/world
  ├── Distorted blame of self or others
  ├── Persistent negative emotional state (fear, horror, anger, guilt, shame)
  ├── Diminished interest in activities
  ├── Feeling detached/estranged
  └── Inability to experience positive emotions

CRITERION E: Arousal and reactivity changes (≥2 of 6)
  ├── Irritable behavior / angry outbursts
  ├── Reckless or self-destructive behavior
  ├── Hypervigilance
  ├── Exaggerated startle response
  ├── Concentration problems
  └── Sleep disturbance

Duration >1 month; causes significant distress/impairment
Specifiers: with dissociative symptoms; with delayed expression (≥6 months)
```

**Neurobiology:**
```
PTSD = IMPAIRED FEAR EXTINCTION
══════════════════════════════════

  AMYGDALA: HYPERACTIVE ──► Exaggerated fear response to reminders
      │
  HIPPOCAMPUS: HYPOACTIVE ──► Poor contextualization
      │                        ("always dangerous" not "was dangerous THEN")
      │
  vmPFC: HYPOACTIVE ──► Failed fear extinction; persistent re-experiencing

Neuroendocrine paradox:
  ├── Cortisol: LOWER in PTSD (unlike depression) — ↑ GR sensitivity
  ├── Catecholamines (NE): ELEVATED — locus coeruleus hyperactivity
  └── CRH: ELEVATED in CSF
```

**Treatment:**
| Modality | Therapy | Evidence |
|----------|---------|----------|
| First-line psychotherapy | CPT (Cognitive Processing Therapy) | Strong A |
| First-line psychotherapy | PE (Prolonged Exposure) | Strong A |
| First-line psychotherapy | EMDR | Strong A |
| First-line medication | Sertraline (FDA-approved) | A |
| First-line medication | Paroxetine (FDA-approved) | A |
| Nightmares | Prazosin (α1-blocker) | B |
| Emerging | MDMA-assisted therapy | Phase 3 positive |
| Emerging | Psilocybin | Early Phase 2 |

### 7.2 Complex PTSD (ICD-11)

```
All PTSD criteria PLUS "Disturbances in Self-Organization" (DSO):
  1. Affect dysregulation (difficulty calming, emotional numbness or explosivity)
  2. Negative self-concept (persistent shame, guilt, worthlessness)
  3. Disturbances in relationships (difficulty closeness, avoidance, revictimization)

From: prolonged repeated trauma (childhood abuse, domestic violence, torture)

Treatment: phased approach:
  Phase 1: Safety, stabilization, affect regulation (DBT skills, grounding)
  Phase 2: Trauma processing (CPT, PE, EMDR — adapted)
  Phase 3: Reconnection, rehabilitation, identity consolidation
```

### 7.3 Other Trauma/Stressor-Related Disorders

- **Acute Stress Disorder:** Similar to PTSD but 3 days to 1 month; ≥9/14 symptoms; ~50% progress to PTSD
- **Adjustment Disorders:** Symptoms within 3 months of stressor; out of proportion; subtypes: depressed, anxious, mixed, disturbed conduct
- **Reactive Attachment Disorder / Disinhibited Social Engagement Disorder:** Children with pathogenic care (neglect); inhibited vs indiscriminate sociability
- **Moral Injury:** Not DSM; psychological damage from violating moral code; guilt, shame, existential crisis

## 8. Obsessive-Compulsive and Related Disorders

### 8.1 Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)

**Epidemiology:** Lifetime prevalence ~2-3%; equal gender ratio in adults; mean onset ~20 years (bimodal: ~10 and ~21)

**Common Symptom Dimensions:**

| Dimension | Obsession Examples | Compulsion Examples |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| **Contamination** | Germs, dirt, bodily fluids, illness | Hand washing, cleaning, avoidance |
| **Harm** | Hurting self/others, causing accidents | Checking (locks, stove), reassurance |
| **Symmetry/Ordering** | Things must be "just right" | Arranging, counting, repeating |
| **Forbidden thoughts** | Sexual, violent, blasphemous | Mental rituals, reassurance, avoidance |
| **Hoarding** | Need to save; distress at discarding | Acquiring, difficulty discarding |

**Y-BOCS (Yale-Brown OCD Scale):** 10 items (0-40); Mild: 8-15 | Moderate: 16-23 | Severe: 24-31 | Extreme: 32-40

### 8.2 CSTC Circuit in OCD

```
CORTICO-STRIATO-THALAMO-CORTICAL (CSTC) LOOP
═════════════════════════════════════════════

  ┌──────────────────┐
  │   OFC / ACC      │ ◄──── Excessive cortical drive (obsessions)
  │   (HYPERACTIVE)  │
  └────────┬─────────┘
           ▼
  ┌──────────────────┐
  │  CAUDATE NUCLEUS  │ ──── Impaired filtering/gating
  │  (HYPERACTIVE)   │
  └────────┬─────────┘
           ▼ Direct pathway dominates
  ┌──────────────────┐
  │  THALAMUS        │ ──── Gate stuck open → loop perseverates
  │  (HYPERACTIVE)   │
  └────────┬─────────┘
           └──► back to Cortex (feeds the loop)

TREATMENT EFFECTS:
  ├── SSRIs: normalize caudate hyperactivity
  ├── ERP: reduces OFC-caudate hyperconnectivity
  └── DBS: disrupts pathological circuit activity
```

### 8.3 OCD Treatment

```
STEP 1: ERP + SSRI (high dose: fluoxetine 40-80mg, sertraline 200mg)
  ├── Allow 8-12 weeks (longer than for depression)
  └── Combination ERP + SSRI superior to either alone

STEP 2: Clomipramine (most serotonergic TCA; 150-250 mg)
  └── May be most effective single drug; limited by side effects

STEP 3: Augmentation
  ├── Atypical antipsychotic: aripiprazole (5-15 mg), risperidone (0.5-2 mg)
  ├── Glutamate modulators: NAC 2400-3000 mg, memantine 10-20 mg
  └── D-cycloserine to enhance ERP extinction learning

STEP 4: Refractory OCD
  ├── DBS (ventral capsule/ventral striatum) — humanitarian device exemption
  ├── Gamma knife capsulotomy (severe refractory)
  └── TMS (supplementary motor area targeting)
```

| Disorder | Prevalence | Key Features | Treatment |
|----------|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Body Dysmorphic Disorder** | 1.7-2.9% | Preoccupation with perceived appearance flaw; mirror checking, reassurance | SSRI (high dose) + CBT |
| **Hoarding Disorder** | 2-6% | Difficulty discarding; cluttered living spaces | CBT for hoarding; SSRIs (modest) |
| **Trichotillomania** | 1-2% | Recurrent hair pulling → hair loss | HRT (habit reversal training); NAC |

## 9. Bipolar and Related Disorders

### 9.1 Bipolar I Disorder

**Epidemiology:** Lifetime prevalence ~1%; equal gender ratio; mean onset ~18-25 years; heritability ~80%

**Manic Episode Criteria (required for Bipolar I):**

```
Duration: ≥1 week (or ANY duration if hospitalization needed)
Mood: Abnormally elevated, expansive, OR irritable + ↑ energy/activity

≥3 symptoms (4 if mood is only irritable):

Mnemonic: DIG FAST

D — Distractibility
I — Impulsivity / Indiscretion (spending sprees, sexual indiscretions)
G — Grandiosity (inflated self-esteem; may be delusional)
F — Flight of ideas / racing thoughts
A — Activity increase (goal-directed or psychomotor agitation)
S — Sleep decreased (feels rested after 3 hours)
T — Talkativeness / pressured speech

SEVERITY: Marked functional impairment, psychotic features, or hospitalization
One lifetime manic episode = Bipolar I (even without depression)
```

### 9.2 Bipolar II Disorder

**Hypomania vs Mania:**

| Feature | Hypomania | Mania |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| Duration | ≥4 consecutive days | ≥7 days (or any if hospitalized) |
| Severity | Observable change but NOT marked impairment | Marked impairment |
| Psychotic features | NEVER | May be present |
| Hospitalization | Not required | Often required |

BP-II is NOT milder than BP-I: more time depressed (~50x more depression than hypomania), high suicide risk, average 5-10 years to correct diagnosis

### 9.3 Neurobiology of Bipolar Disorder

```
KEY MECHANISMS:
  1. MITOCHONDRIAL DYSFUNCTION → ↓ ATP → ↑ oxidative stress
  2. CIRCADIAN RHYTHM DISRUPTION → Clock gene polymorphisms; sleep deprivation triggers mania
  3. KINDLING MODEL (Post, 1992) → Episodes beget episodes; lower threshold over time
  4. CALCIUM SIGNALING → CACNA1C (top GWAS hit across mood disorders)
  5. NEUROINFLAMMATION → ↑ CRP, IL-6 during episodes
```

### 9.4 Mood Stabilizer Comparison

| Drug | Acute Mania | Bipolar Depression | Maintenance | Key Side Effects | Monitoring |
|------|-------------|-------------------|-------------|-----------------|------------|
| **Lithium** | ++ | + | +++ (gold standard) | Tremor, polyuria, hypothyroidism (20%), renal, weight gain, teratogen (Ebstein's 0.1%) | Li level q3-6mo (0.6-1.2 mEq/L), TSH, creatinine |
| **Valproate** | +++ | +/- | ++ | Weight gain, hair loss, hepatotoxicity, pancreatitis, teratogen (NTDs — AVOID in women of childbearing age) | VPA level (50-125), LFTs, CBC |
| **Carbamazepine** | ++ | +/- | + | Rash (SJS — HLA-B*1502 test), hyponatremia, aplastic anemia, autoinduction | CBZ level (4-12), CBC, Na⁺, HLA pre-test |
| **Lamotrigine** | ✗ | +++ | ++ (depression prevention) | Rash (SJS 0.08% — SLOW titration mandatory) | Titrate 25 mg/2wk → 200 mg over 6+ weeks |

### 9.5 Bipolar Depression Treatment

```
PREFERRED (avoid antidepressant monotherapy → risk of manic switch):
  1. Quetiapine 300 mg — FDA-approved
  2. Lurasidone 20-120 mg — FDA-approved
  3. Lamotrigine 200 mg — strong evidence; slow titration
  4. Olanzapine/fluoxetine (Symbyax) — FDA-approved; metabolic concerns
  5. Cariprazine 1.5-3 mg — FDA-approved
  6. Lithium — evidence supports
  7. ECT — highly effective for severe bipolar depression
```

## 10. Schizophrenia Spectrum Disorders

### 10.1 Schizophrenia

**Epidemiology:**
- Lifetime prevalence: ~0.7-1%
- Equal gender ratio (men: earlier onset ~18-25; women: ~25-35)
- Life expectancy: 15-20 years shorter than general population
- Suicide risk: ~5%

**Symptom Domains:**

```
POSITIVE SYMPTOMS (respond best to antipsychotics):
  ├── Hallucinations (auditory most common: 60-80%)
  ├── Delusions (persecutory, referential, grandiose, control)
  ├── Disorganized speech (tangentiality, derailment, word salad)
  └── Disorganized/catatonic behavior

NEGATIVE SYMPTOMS (respond poorly to typical antipsychotics):
  ├── Avolition (↓ motivation)
  ├── Alogia (poverty of speech)
  ├── Anhedonia (↓ pleasure)
  ├── Flat affect (↓ emotional expression)
  └── Asociality (↓ social interest)

COGNITIVE SYMPTOMS (present in 75-85%; BEST predictor of functional outcome):
  ├── Working memory deficits
  ├── Attention/vigilance impairment
  ├── Processing speed reduction
  └── Executive function deficits
```

**DSM-5-TR:** ≥2 of (delusions, hallucinations, disorganized speech, disorganized behavior, negative symptoms) for significant portion of 1 month; at least one must be delusions, hallucinations, or disorganized speech; duration ≥6 months; functional decline.

### 10.2 Neurobiology

```
1. DOPAMINE HYPOTHESIS
   MESOLIMBIC: OVERACTIVE → positive symptoms
   MESOCORTICAL: UNDERACTIVE → negative/cognitive symptoms
   Evidence: amphetamine → psychosis; all antipsychotics block D2

2. GLUTAMATE/NMDA HYPOTHESIS
   NMDA hypofunction → disinhibition → downstream DA dysregulation
   PCP/ketamine → positive AND negative AND cognitive symptoms

3. NEURODEVELOPMENTAL MODEL
   Prenatal insult → abnormal development → excessive synaptic pruning
   (C4A complement gene) in adolescence → symptom emergence

4. NEUROINFLAMMATION
   Microglial activation; ↑ CRP, IL-6; MHC region = strongest GWAS signal
```


## 11. Neurodevelopmental Disorders

### 11.1 ADHD (Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder)

**Epidemiology:** Children: 5-10%; Adults: 2.5-4.4%; M:F = 2:1 children (1.6:1 adults — women underdiagnosed); onset before age 12

**DSM-5-TR Presentations:**

```
INATTENTIVE (≥6 of 9 for children; ≥5 for adults ≥17):
  1. Fails to give close attention / careless mistakes
  2. Difficulty sustaining attention in tasks/play
  3. Does not seem to listen when spoken to directly
  4. Fails to follow through on instructions / finish tasks
  5. Difficulty organizing tasks and activities
  6. Avoids tasks requiring sustained mental effort
  7. Loses things necessary for tasks
  8. Easily distracted by extraneous stimuli
  9. Forgetful in daily activities

HYPERACTIVE-IMPULSIVE (≥6 of 9 for children; ≥5 for adults ≥17):
  1. Fidgets or squirms
  2. Leaves seat when remaining seated is expected
  3. Runs/climbs in inappropriate situations (restlessness in adults)
  4. Unable to play/engage quietly
  5. "On the go" / "driven by a motor"
  6. Talks excessively
  7. Blurts out answers before questions are completed
  8. Difficulty waiting turn
  9. Interrupts or intrudes on others

COMBINED: meets both inattentive and hyperactive-impulsive criteria
```

**Neurobiology:** Frontostriatal circuit dysfunction; catecholamine (DA/NE) deficiency in PFC; delayed cortical maturation (~3 years behind peers); default mode network intrusion into task-positive states

**Medication Comparison:**

| Drug | Class | Mechanism | Duration | Starting Dose | Key Notes |
|------|-------|-----------|----------|---------------|-----------|
| **Methylphenidate IR** | Stimulant | DA/NE reuptake inhibition | 3-4 h | 5 mg BID-TID | Ritalin; generic; flexible dosing |
| **Methylphenidate ER** | Stimulant | DA/NE reuptake inhibition | 8-12 h | 18-36 mg daily | Concerta, Ritalin LA |

### 11.2 Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)

**Epidemiology:** ~1 in 36 children (CDC 2023); M:F = 3-4:1 (girls underdiagnosed — "camouflaging"); onset in early development

**DSM-5-TR Criteria:**
- **A.** Persistent deficits in social communication and social interaction (all 3):
  - Deficits in social-emotional reciprocity
  - Deficits in nonverbal communicative behaviors
  - Deficits in developing/maintaining relationships
- **B.** Restricted, repetitive behaviors (≥2 of 4):
  - Stereotyped/repetitive motor movements, speech, or use of objects
  - Insistence on sameness, routines, ritualized patterns
  - Highly restricted, fixated interests
  - Hyper- or hyporeactivity to sensory input

**Severity Levels:**
| Level | Social Communication | Restricted/Repetitive |
|-------|---------------------|----------------------|
| Level 1: "Requiring support" | Difficulty initiating; atypical responses | Inflexibility causes interference; difficulty switching |
| Level 2: "Requiring substantial support" | Marked deficits; limited initiation; reduced/abnormal responses | Obvious to casual observer; distress with change |
| Level 3: "Requiring very substantial support" | Severe deficits; very limited initiation; minimal response | Marked interference; extreme difficulty with change |

**Comorbidities:** Anxiety (40%), ADHD (30-60%), epilepsy (20-30%), GI issues (50%), sleep problems (50-80%), intellectual disability (33%)

**Treatment:** ABA (applied behavior analysis), speech therapy, occupational therapy, social skills training; no FDA-approved medications for core symptoms; medications target comorbidities (risperidone/aripiprazole FDA-approved for irritability in ASD)

### 12.3 Borderline Personality Disorder — Deep Dive

**DSM-5-TR Criteria (≥5 of 9):**
1. Frantic efforts to avoid real or imagined abandonment
2. Unstable and intense interpersonal relationships (idealization/devaluation)
3. Identity disturbance (unstable self-image or sense of self)
4. Impulsivity in ≥2 areas (spending, sex, substance use, reckless driving, binge eating)
5. Recurrent suicidal behavior, gestures, threats, or self-harm
6. Affective instability (intense episodic dysphoria, irritability, anxiety; lasting hours-days)
7. Chronic feelings of emptiness
8. Inappropriate intense anger or difficulty controlling anger
9. Transient stress-related paranoid ideation or severe dissociative symptoms

**Neurobiology:** Amygdala hyperreactivity, reduced prefrontal inhibition, ↓ hippocampal/amygdala volume, oxytocin dysregulation, HPA axis hyperreactivity

**Evidence-Based Treatments:**

| Therapy | Developer | Core Principle | Structure |
|---------|-----------|---------------|-----------|
| **DBT** | Marsha Linehan | Dialectical: acceptance + change; biosocial theory | Individual + skills group + phone coaching + consultation team |
| **MBT** | Bateman & Fonagy | Mentalization: understanding behavior in terms of mental states | Group + individual; partially hospitalized format |
| **Schema Therapy** | Jeffrey Young | Early maladaptive schemas + coping modes | Individual; reparenting; mode work |
| **TFP** | Kernberg | Object relations; transference analysis | Individual 2×/week; therapist as "new object" |

**DBT Four Modules:**
```
1. CORE MINDFULNESS — "Wise mind" (intersection of rational + emotional mind)
2. DISTRESS TOLERANCE — Crisis survival skills (TIPP, ACCEPTS, IMPROVE)
3. EMOTION REGULATION — Understanding and managing emotions; opposite action
4. INTERPERSONAL EFFECTIVENESS — DEAR MAN (assertiveness), GIVE (relationship), FAST (self-respect)
```

### 12.4 ICD-11 Dimensional Model

```
ICD-11 replaced categorical PDs with a DIMENSIONAL model:

1. Rate SEVERITY: Mild → Moderate → Severe (based on functioning)

2. Rate TRAIT DOMAINS:
   ├── Negative Affectivity (emotional instability, anxiety, separation insecurity)
   ├── Detachment (social withdrawal, restricted affect, anhedonia)
   ├── Dissociality (callousness, deceitfulness, hostility, grandiosity)
   ├── Disinhibition (impulsivity, irresponsibility, recklessness)
   └── Anankastia (perfectionism, rigidity, emotional constriction)

3. Optional "Borderline Pattern" qualifier (retained due to clinical utility)
```

## 13. Substance Use Disorders and Addiction

### 13.1 Neurobiology of Addiction

```
MESOLIMBIC DOPAMINE (REWARD) PATHWAY
═════════════════════════════════════

  VTA (Ventral Tegmental Area)
    │
    │  Dopamine neurons
    │
    ▼
  NAcc (Nucleus Accumbens)  ◄──── Reward, pleasure, "wanting"
    │                              All addictive substances ↑ DA here
    │                              Natural rewards: food ↑50-100%
    │                              Cocaine: ↑300-400%
    │                              Methamphetamine: ↑1000-1200%
    ▼
  PFC (Prefrontal Cortex)  ◄──── Decision-making, impulse control
    │                              IMPAIRED in addiction → loss of control
    │
    └──► Amygdala, hippocampus ◄── Contextual cues, emotional memory
                                    → trigger cravings long after abstinence

THREE-STAGE MODEL (Koob & Volkow):
  1. BINGE/INTOXICATION: ↑ DA in NAcc → reward, positive reinforcement
  2. WITHDRAWAL/NEGATIVE AFFECT: ↓ DA, ↑ stress hormones (CRH, dynorphin)
     → dysphoria, anxiety → negative reinforcement (use to feel normal)
  3. PREOCCUPATION/ANTICIPATION: PFC dysfunction → impaired decision-making
     → compulsive seeking; cue-triggered cravings (hippocampus/amygdala)
```

### 13.2 DSM-5-TR Substance Use Disorder Criteria

```
≥2 of 11 criteria in 12-month period:

IMPAIRED CONTROL:
  1. Taken in larger amounts or longer than intended
  2. Persistent desire or unsuccessful efforts to cut down
  3. Great deal of time spent obtaining, using, or recovering
  4. Craving (strong desire or urge to use)

SOCIAL IMPAIRMENT:
  5. Failure to fulfill major role obligations (work, school, home)
  6. Continued use despite persistent social/interpersonal problems
  7. Important activities given up or reduced

RISKY USE:
  8. Recurrent use in physically hazardous situations
  9. Continued use despite knowledge of physical/psychological problems

PHARMACOLOGICAL:
  10. Tolerance (need more for same effect; diminished effect with same amount)
  11. Withdrawal (characteristic syndrome; substance taken to relieve/avoid)

Severity: Mild (2-3) | Moderate (4-5) | Severe (6+)
```

### 13.3 Substance-Specific Profiles

| Substance | Screening | Withdrawal Timeline | FDA-Approved Treatments | Key Facts |
|-----------|-----------|--------------------|-----------------------|-----------|
| **Alcohol** | AUDIT, CAGE | 6-24h: tremor, anxiety, sweating; 24-48h: seizure risk; 48-72h: delirium tremens | Naltrexone (oral/IM), acamprosate, disulfiram | CIWA-Ar for withdrawal management; DTs mortality 5-15% untreated |
| **Opioids** | DAST-10 | 8-24h: lacrimation, rhinorrhea, yawning; 36-72h: peak (cramps, diarrhea, mydriasis) | Methadone, buprenorphine/naloxone (Suboxone), naltrexone XR (Vivitrol) | MAT saves lives; fentanyl crisis; COWS scale |
| **Tobacco/Nicotine** | Fagerstrom | Hours-days: irritability, anxiety, ↑ appetite, difficulty concentrating | Varenicline (Chantix), bupropion, NRT (patch, gum, lozenge, inhaler, nasal spray) | #1 preventable cause of death; varenicline most effective |
| **Cannabis** | CUDIT | Irritability, insomnia, ↓ appetite, anxiety (peaks day 2-3, lasts 1-2 weeks) | None FDA-approved | CUD affects ~10% of users; synthetic cannabinoids more dangerous |
| **Cocaine/Stimulants** | — | "Crash": fatigue, hypersomnia, depression, ↑ appetite (no medically dangerous withdrawal) | None FDA-approved | Contingency management best behavioral Rx; methylphenidate trials ongoing |
| **Benzodiazepines** | — | 1-4 days (short-acting) or 2-7 days (long-acting): anxiety, tremor, seizures, psychosis | Cross-taper to long-acting (diazepam/chlordiazepoxide), then slow taper | Can be FATAL — like alcohol withdrawal; taper over weeks-months |

### 14.2 Medical Complications of Anorexia Nervosa

```
CARDIAC:   Bradycardia, QTc prolongation, mitral valve prolapse, heart failure
ENDOCRINE: Amenorrhea, ↓ estrogen, ↓ testosterone, ↓ T3 (sick euthyroid),
           ↑ cortisol, ↑ GH (resistance), osteoporosis
METABOLIC: Refeeding syndrome (↓ phosphate → cardiac arrest — monitor closely),
           hypoglycemia, hypokalemia, metabolic alkalosis (purging)
HEMATOLOGIC: Pancytopenia (bone marrow suppression)
GI:        Gastroparesis, constipation, SMA syndrome, parotid enlargement (purging)
DENTAL:    Enamel erosion (perimolysis — from vomiting)
OTHER:     Lanugo hair, Russell's sign (knuckle calluses from purging),
           hypothermia, edema
```

### 14.3 Refeeding Syndrome

```
Occurs when malnourished patients receive nutrition too rapidly:
  ├── ↓ Phosphate (most dangerous) → cardiac arrhythmias, respiratory failure, death
  ├── ↓ Potassium, ↓ magnesium
  ├── Fluid shifts → edema, heart failure
  └── Thiamine deficiency → Wernicke's encephalopathy

Prevention:
  ├── "Start low, go slow" — begin at 1000-1200 kcal/day
  ├── Supplement phosphate, potassium, magnesium, thiamine BEFORE feeding
  ├── Monitor electrolytes BID for first 5 days
  └── Increase calories by 200-300 kcal every 2-3 days
```

## 15. Sleep-Wake Disorders

### 15.1 Insomnia Disorder

**Prevalence:** 6-10% (chronic insomnia); 30-35% (insomnia symptoms)

**Treatment:**

```
GOLD STANDARD: CBT-I (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Components:
  1. SLEEP RESTRICTION: Limit time in bed to actual sleep time → builds sleep drive
  2. STIMULUS CONTROL: Bed for sleep/sex only; get up if awake >20 min
  3. COGNITIVE RESTRUCTURING: Challenge catastrophic beliefs about sleep
  4. SLEEP HYGIENE: Consistent wake time, dark/cool room, no screens before bed
  5. RELAXATION TRAINING: Progressive muscle relaxation, diaphragmatic breathing

Pharmacotherapy (second-line or adjunct):
  ├── Orexin antagonists: suvorexant (Belsomra), lemborexant (Dayvigo)
  │     └── Block wake-promoting orexin → promote sleep; less dependence risk
  ├── Doxepin (low-dose 3-6 mg): H1 antagonist; FDA-approved for insomnia
  ├── Ramelteon: melatonin MT1/MT2 agonist; no abuse potential; sleep onset
  ├── Z-drugs: zolpidem, eszopiclone, zaleplon — GABA-A modulation
  │     └── Short-term only; parasomnias (sleepwalking, sleep-driving), dependence
  └── Avoid: benzodiazepines for chronic insomnia (tolerance, dependence, falls)
```

### 15.2 Other Sleep Disorders

| Disorder | Key Features | Treatment |
|----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Narcolepsy Type 1** | Excessive daytime sleepiness + cataplexy (sudden muscle weakness triggered by emotion); orexin/hypocretin deficiency | Sodium oxybate (Xyrem), modafinil, pitolisant, solriamfetol |
| **Narcolepsy Type 2** | EDS without cataplexy; normal CSF orexin | Modafinil, stimulants |

### 16.4 Delirium

```
DELIRIUM: Acute, fluctuating disturbance of attention and awareness
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Subtypes:
  ├── Hyperactive (agitated, pulling lines, combative) — 25%
  ├── Hypoactive (quiet, withdrawn, lethargic) — 50% — most underdiagnosed
  └── Mixed — 25%

Causes (mnemonic: I WATCH DEATH):
  I — Infection (UTI, pneumonia, sepsis)
  W — Withdrawal (alcohol, benzodiazepines, opioids)
```

### 17.3 Drug-Drug Interactions in Psychiatry

```
SEROTONIN SYNDROME (Hunter Criteria — ANY of these groups):
═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
In presence of a serotonergic agent:
  1. Spontaneous clonus
  2. Inducible clonus + (agitation OR diaphoresis)
  3. Ocular clonus + (agitation OR diaphoresis)
  4. Tremor + hyperreflexia
  5. Hypertonia + temperature >38°C + (ocular clonus OR inducible clonus)

Dangerous combinations:
  ├── SSRI + MAOI (CONTRAINDICATED — wait 2 weeks washout; 5 weeks for fluoxetine)
  ├── SSRI + tramadol / meperidine / fentanyl
  ├── SSRI + linezolid (MAOI activity) or methylene blue
  ├── SSRI + triptans (sumatriptan) — FDA warning but risk is low
  └── SSRI + St. John's wort / lithium / tryptophan

Treatment: Stop serotonergic agents; cyproheptadine 12mg then 4mg q2h;
  benzodiazepines; cooling; supportive care

NEUROLEPTIC MALIGNANT SYNDROME (NMS):
  Tetrad: hyperthermia + rigidity + autonomic instability + altered mental status
  CK often >1000 IU/L; WBC elevated; metabolic acidosis
  Treatment: stop antipsychotic; dantrolene 1-2.5 mg/kg IV; bromocriptine 2.5 mg q8h
  Mortality: 10-20% if untreated

QTc PROLONGATION — HIGH-RISK PSYCHOTROPICS:
  ├── Thioridazine, pimozide, IV haloperidol, ziprasidone
  ├── Citalopram >40 mg (FDA limit), escitalopram >20 mg
  ├── Methadone (dose-dependent)
  └── TCAs (especially in overdose)
  QTc >500 ms → high risk of Torsades de Pointes → stop offending agent
```

```

  ├── AVOID: TCAs, benzodiazepines, anticholinergics, first-gen antipsychotics
  ├── AVOID: chlorpromazine, thioridazine (orthostatic, anticholinergic)

  ├── Gabapentin, pregabalin: dose adjustment needed
```

## 19. Neuromodulation and Brain Stimulation

### 19.1 Comparison of Modalities

| Modality | Invasiveness | Mechanism | FDA-Cleared Indications | Response Rate | Key Side Effects |
|----------|-------------|-----------|------------------------|---------------|------------------|
| **ECT** | Anesthesia required | Electrically induced generalized seizure | TRD, catatonia, acute suicidality, psychotic depression | 50-70% (TRD); 80-90% (catatonia) | Retrograde amnesia (usually transient), headache, nausea |
| **rTMS** | Non-invasive | Magnetic field → focused cortical stimulation | TRD, OCD, anxious depression, smoking cessation | 30-60% (TRD) | Headache, scalp pain; seizure (<0.1%) |
| **SNT/SAINT** | Non-invasive | Accelerated theta-burst TMS (50 sessions over 5 days) | Investigational (FDA breakthrough) | ~80% (open-label; ~60% RCT) | Same as TMS; requires neuroimaging targeting |
| **VNS** | Implanted device | Vagus nerve electrical stimulation → affects NE, 5-HT, brainstem nuclei | TRD (adjunctive), epilepsy | 30-40% (over 12+ months; slow onset) | Voice hoarseness, cough, dyspnea; surgical risks |
| **DBS** | Neurosurgical implant | Chronic stimulation of specific brain targets | Investigational for TRD; humanitarian device for OCD | Variable (30-60% in small trials) | Surgical risks, device complications, mood changes |
| **tDCS** | Non-invasive | Weak direct current modulates cortical excitability | Not FDA-cleared (investigational) | Modest effect sizes | Scalp irritation, tingling |

### 19.2 ECT Details

```
ELECTRODE PLACEMENT:
  ├── Right unilateral (RUL): fewer cognitive effects; higher charge needed
  ├── Bilateral (bitemporal): most effective; more amnesia
  └── Bifrontal: compromise approach

PROTOCOL:
  ├── Anesthesia: methohexital or propofol (short-acting)
  ├── Muscle relaxant: succinylcholine (prevents fractures)
  ├── Seizure duration: target 25-60 seconds (motor); EEG monitoring
  ├── Frequency: 2-3× per week; acute course: 6-12 sessions
  └── Maintenance ECT: monthly (prevents relapse)

INDICATIONS (strongest evidence):
  ├── Severe depression with acute suicidality (fastest acting treatment)
  ├── Catatonia (response rate >80%)
  ├── Psychotic depression (superior to medication alone)
  ├── Treatment-resistant depression (failed 2+ medications)
  └── Mania (refractory to medications)
```

### 19.3 TMS Protocols

```
STANDARD rTMS:
  ├── Target: left DLPFC (depression) — 10 Hz high-frequency stimulation
  ├── Session: 37.5 minutes; 3000 pulses/session
  ├── Course: 5 days/week × 4-6 weeks (20-30 sessions)
  └── Maintenance: variable; some patients need periodic "booster" sessions

THETA-BURST STIMULATION (TBS):
  ├── Intermittent TBS (iTBS): excitatory; 3 min/session
  ├── FDA-cleared 2018 for depression (non-inferior to standard rTMS)
  └── Much shorter session time

STANFORD NEUROMODULATION THERAPY (SNT/SAINT):
  ├── 10 sessions/day × 5 days = 50 sessions in 1 week
  ├── Individualized targeting using fMRI connectivity (sgACC-DLPFC anticorrelation)
  ├── ~80% remission in open-label study (Nolan Williams, Stanford)
  └── Paradigm shift: accelerated treatment, personalized targeting

DEEP TMS (BrainsWay H-coil):
  ├── Penetrates deeper brain structures
  ├── FDA-cleared: MDD (2013), OCD (2018), smoking cessation (2020), anxious depression (2021)
  └── OCD target: medial PFC/ACC
```

## 20. Psychiatric Emergencies

### 20.1 Suicide Risk Assessment

```
RISK FACTORS (mnemonic: SAD PERSONS):
  S — Sex (male → higher completion; female → higher attempts)
  A — Age (<25 and >65 highest risk)
  D — Depression / other psychiatric disorder
  P — Previous attempt (strongest single predictor)
  E — Ethanol / substance use
  R — Rational thinking loss (psychosis)
  S — Social support lacking
  O — Organized plan (specific method, means, timeline)
  N — No spouse / unmarried
  S — Sickness (chronic pain, terminal illness)

ACUTE ASSESSMENT:
  ├── Suicidal ideation: active (with intent/plan) vs passive ("wish I were dead")
  ├── Intent: desire to act on thoughts
  ├── Plan: specific method, means access, timeline, preparations
  ├── Means: lethality and accessibility (firearms = highest lethality)
  ├── Protective factors: reasons for living, social support, children, religious beliefs
  └── Hopelessness: strongest cognitive predictor (Beck Hopelessness Scale)

SCREENING TOOLS:
  ├── C-SSRS (Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale): gold standard structured assessment
  ├── PHQ-9 Item 9: "thoughts that you would be better off dead"
  ├── ASQ (Ask Suicide-Screening Questions): 4 questions, ED validated
  └── SAFE-T: 5-step evaluation and triage

SAFETY PLANNING (Stanley-Brown):
  1. Warning signs that crisis is developing
  2. Internal coping strategies (distraction, self-soothing)
  3. People and social settings that provide distraction
  4. People to ask for help (family, friends)
  5. Professionals and agencies to contact (988 Suicide Lifeline, crisis services)
  6. Making the environment safe (lethal means restriction)
```

### 20.2 Acute Psychosis Management

```
VERBAL DE-ESCALATION:
  ├── Speak calmly, slowly, with low volume
  ├── Respect personal space (arm's length)
  ├── Avoid confrontation, threats, or arguing with delusions
  ├── Offer choices ("Would you like to sit here or there?")
  ├── Validate emotion without agreeing with content
  └── Set clear, simple limits

PHARMACOLOGICAL (if de-escalation fails):
  ├── First-line oral: olanzapine 5-10 mg ODT, risperidone 2 mg, haloperidol 5 mg
  ├── IM options (agitation):
  │     ├── Olanzapine 10 mg IM (do NOT combine with IM benzodiazepine → respiratory depression)
  │     ├── Haloperidol 5 mg + lorazepam 2 mg + diphenhydramine 50 mg IM ("B52")
  │     ├── Ziprasidone 10-20 mg IM
  │     └── Midazolam 5 mg IM (fastest onset for severe agitation)
  └── Monitor: respiratory status, vital signs, temperature
```

### 20.3 Catatonia

```
SIGNS (Bush-Francis Catatonia Rating Scale — 23 items):
  ├── Motor: immobility, stupor, mutism, staring, posturing, waxy flexibility
  ├── Excitement: excessive purposeless motor activity
  ├── Echolalia (repeating words), echopraxia (repeating movements)
  ├── Negativism, stereotypy, mannerisms, rigidity
  └── Autonomic: tachycardia, hypertension, diaphoresis (malignant catatonia)

LORAZEPAM CHALLENGE TEST:
  ├── Give lorazepam 1-2 mg IV/IM
  ├── Observe for improvement in 5-10 minutes
  ├── Positive response = catatonia confirmed
  └── Continue lorazepam 2-16 mg/day in divided doses

ECT: For malignant catatonia, catatonia not responsive to benzodiazepines
  └── Highly effective; can be life-saving in malignant catatonia

AVOID: antipsychotics in undiagnosed catatonia → risk of NMS
```

### 20.4 Alcohol Withdrawal

```
TIMELINE:
  6-12h:    Tremor, anxiety, headache, nausea, diaphoresis, insomnia
  12-24h:   Alcoholic hallucinosis (visual/auditory/tactile — consciousness intact)
  24-48h:   Withdrawal SEIZURES (generalized tonic-clonic; 2-5% of withdrawals)
  48-96h:   DELIRIUM TREMENS (confusion, agitation, fever, autonomic instability,
            hallucinations; mortality 5-15% untreated, <1% treated)

CIWA-Ar (Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment — Revised):
  10 items scored 0-7 each; max 67
  ├── <10: mild → reassess, may not need benzos
  ├── 10-18: moderate → benzodiazepine per protocol
  └── >20: severe → aggressive treatment; ICU consideration

PROTOCOLS:
  ├── Symptom-triggered: lorazepam or chlordiazepoxide PRN based on CIWA score
  │   └── Less total benzos, shorter treatment, fewer complications
  ├── Fixed-dose taper: chlordiazepoxide on schedule with PRN supplements
  └── Adjuncts: thiamine 100mg IV BEFORE glucose (prevent Wernicke's), folate, Mg²⁺
```

### 22.2 Nutritional Psychiatry

```

  ├── Mediterranean diet: 30-35% ↓ depression risk (meta-analysis)

  │         to treatment → NNT = 4.1 for remission

  ├── Gut microbiome composition differs in depression, anxiety, schizophrenia, autism
  ├── Microbiome → vagus nerve + immune system + metabolites → brain
  ├── ↓ Lactobacillus, ↓ Bifidobacterium in depression
```
